Where are the gods? (A short story by chase proctor)
One day, there were three men, born in slavery in Egypt. They grew up moving and shaping stones every day, being told that if they worked hard and diligently their whole lives, the gods would grant them pleasure in the next life, at the end of days.
As they struggled in the heat of the day, pulling a stone through the sand, over a bed of logs, the stone caught on a rotten piece of wood, and fell off it's track. The Slave master noticed the halt in their progress and came to the threre men with his wip, and he cracked it at the first man, noticing that he had stumbled. In anger, the slave rebuked his tormenter. It is not I who have caused this: the stone caught a rotten piece and fell. The man whipped him again, saying that stones did not move on their own accord. At this, the others came to the aid of their friend and defended him, confirming his story. The Slaver begrudgingly accepted their plea and bid them to move forward once they had restrored the stone to its track.
As he turned to leave, one of the men asked him, "If all of us work to please the gods, what pleasure do they take from your whip?". This made the slaver furious, and he raised his arm to strike the man. He dropped it with an exhalation and said, simply, "each of us is given a station, determined by the gods. Yours is to toil and sweat. Mine is to protect the world from your laziness and complacency. I can assume only, that they enjoy your fatigue as much as my justice, so carry on your way, and leave me to my higher doings." With this, he deaprted from them.
